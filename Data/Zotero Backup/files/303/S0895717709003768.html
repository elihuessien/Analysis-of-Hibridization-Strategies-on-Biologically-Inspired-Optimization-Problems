A time-based integration method of spatio-temporal data at spatial database level

Abstract

The integration of time and space is the key to the research on Temporal Geographic Information System (TGIS). This paper will do some research on the integration method of spatio-temporal data at the spatial database level combining with requirements of cadastral management. Firstly, making analysis on the widely used integration method regarding time as attribute and its corresponding query method, the author thinks that there are some disadvantages on the expression of time semantics and efficiency of querying historical data in this algorithm. Taking these disadvantages into consideration, a new integration method, a time-based integration method, is proposed. In this method, time, as the independent time sequence, becomes the organizational basis for recording changes and intuitionally records the changing process of each spatial entity as time changes. Compared to the method regarding time as attribute, the time-based integration method can express time semantics better and provides a solution for improving backtracking query efficiency by reducing times of backtracking query.

1. Introduction

The key point to the study of TGIS is how to construct a proper spatio-temporal data model for effectively organizing and managing time, space and attribute data, because data organization has direct influence on the approach and efficiency of querying spatio-temporal data [1], [2]. The main difference between spatio-temporal modeling and spatial modeling is the appearance of time stamp when reflecting the realistic world to the database. Therefore, the organization of spatio-temporal data consists of two parts at the database level:

â€¢

Management of spatial data: management of non-structure spatial geometric data and structure attribute data;

â€¢

Integration of spatio-temporal data: integration of temporal data and spatial data.

As the development of spatial database technology, the management of spatial data has undergone three stages: the document management mode, the mixed management mode of document and database, and the integrated management mode of spatial database [3]. In the third stage, the seamless integration and the integrated storage management of spatial geometric data and attribute data are realized by means of spatial database engine (SDE) for the convenience of updating data and storing massive data [4]. Thus, this paper will utilize the integrated mode of spatial database to manage spatial data.

The spatio-temporal integration method is the essence of spatio-temporal data organization. At present, there are two integration methods: regarding time as attribute of the spatial entity and regarding time as a new dimension of the spatial entity. The first one, based on the relational data model, is easier to implement and has been widely used, because the traditional relational data model has rich semantics, more comprehensive theories and much efficient and flexible implementation mechanism. On the contrary, it is still difficult to gain the valid algorithm of constructing high dimension objects and utilize the existing GIS and DBMS to support high dimension objects. Therefore, the integration method regarding time as a new dimension of the spatial entity is still at the stage of academic research [5], [6], [7].

Cadastral information system (CIS) is a typical TGIS. After initial land registration, extremely frequent changing land registration, during which a great many historical data appear, is the main content of cadastral management. In the cadastral management, it is crucial to store and query these historical data. In this respect, it is necessary to study the organization of cadastral spatio-temporal data.

The study will do some research on the integration method of spatio-temporal data at the spatial database level combining with requirements of cadastral management. The paper is organized as follows. Section 2 describes the integration method regarding time as attribute including organization structure of data and corresponding query method, and analyze shortcomings of this method. Taking above shortcomings into consideration, a time-based integration method is proposed and the organization structure of data in this method and corresponding query method is elucidated in Section 3. Based on a specific example, Section 4 will make instantiation analyses of the time-based integration method. In Section 5, the most relevant conclusions are summarized.

2. Integration method regarding time as attribute

The integration method regarding time as attribute, based on relational data model, provides a quick and easy method for solving spatio-temporal problems in GIS. According to different levels of time stamp, three versions appear: relation-level version [8], tuple-level version [9] and attribute-level version [10]. In the relation-level version, any change of spatial entity will produce a new snapshot, which will be stored into relational database. In fact, this is a sequence-snapshot version. In the tuple-level version, every tuple is marked with a time stamp and a new tuple will appear and be marked with new time stamp when changes happen. Every attribute in the tuple is attached with a time stamp in the attribute-level version. A great number of redundant data exist in the first version. Although there is the fewest redundant data in the third version, the use of variable length field is necessary to process these attributes with time stamps. In this respect, it is difficult to realize by relational database. Therefore, tuple-level version is commonly utilized marking method [11].

2.1. Organization structure of spatio-temporal data

This method organizes the spatio-temporal database through constructing event information table and cadastral object information table. The former records time at which changes occur and corresponding specific changes, which expresses the temporal semantics. The later records geometric data and attribute data of each object and relevant events influencing each object, and these events impliedly indicate the life cycle of each object.

â€¢ Event information table

Event information table is used to record temporal semantics, and consists of two aspects: the time changes occur and the specific changes. In the table, a record corresponds to an event taking place at a specific time, and time as attribute is stored in each event tuple. The table records events in the order of time and constitutes the event sequence. Thus, each event tuple can be expressed as formula (1). (1)Event(i)=f(EID,ETi,EAi,EIi)i=(1,2,â€¦,n).

In the formula (1), EID indicates the unique identifier of Event(i), and can be coded in accordance with some principles; ETi expresses the effective time at which Event(i) occurred; EAi is the attribute information of Event(i), describing the types of cadastral events, such as segmentation, mergence and so on; EIi is the descriptive information of Event(i).

â€¢ Cadastral object information table

In this table, cadastral entities are abstracted as objects. Each object has its unique identifier, and its geometric data and attribute data are stored in the same record. Meanwhile, the initial moment and the terminate moment of each object can be indirectly expressed by the moment of events which cause the state of the object to change. The status quo information and the historical information of cadastral objects are stored respectively, so the object information table is also divided into status quo tables and history table. Thus, the object tuple in status quo table and history table can be respectively expressed as formula (2) and formula (3). (2)Object(i)=f(ObjID,ObjSi,ObjAi,OSEi)i=(1,2,â€¦,n)(3)Object(i)=f(ObjID,ObjSi,ObjAi,OSEi,OEEi)i=(12,â€¦,n).

In the formula (2), (3), ObjID is the unique identifier of Object(i) and remains unchanged through its life cycle; ObjSi indicates the geometric location of Object(i); ObjAi expresses the attributes of Object(i), and may contain multiple fields; OSEi and OEEi, respectively, indicate identifiers of the initial event and terminated event which cause the state of Object(i) to change.

The above organization structure of spatio-temporal data is simple and does not store spatio-temporal topological relationships of cadastral objects. Making temporal query on historical data needs to utilize space calculations and spatio-temporal topological relationships (space-intersection and time-meet) between cadastral objects with inheritance relationship. However, the query method will need to do lots of temporal and spatial calculations, and the efficiency will be influenced to some degree with the continuous historical data accumulated. Therefore, many experts have made some improvements on this method and have established the changing relationship table to explicitly record temporal topological relationships between objects with inheritance relationship, that is, inheritance relationships between parent and child objects and changing events, which will avoid a large amount of temporal and spatial calculations and be convenient to query historical data [4], [12], [13]. Thus, each object tuple in the table can be showed as formula (4).(4)Relation(i)=f(FIDi,CIDi,EIDi)i=(1,2,â€¦,n).

In the formula (4), FIDi is the identifier of parent Object, CIDi is the identifier of child Object, EIDi is the identifier of relevant event.

2.2. Shortcomings

At present, the method regarding time as attribute is widely used and offers a shortcut for managing spatio-temporal data. Although this integration method can be used to store and query historical data well, it also has shortcomings:

(1) The expression of time semantics is not explicit. Temporal information is stored dispersedly in each eventâ€™s tuple as attribute rather than structure part of the modeling, which leads to the insufficient expression of time semantics and implicit expression of time sequences.

(2) The process of querying spatio-temporal data is so complicated that further improvements are needed. When spatio-temporal topological relationships are used to query one level of historical data, overlay operation will be executed once and its result will be screened; while the changing relationship table is used to query and backtrack one level of historical data, the entire table will be traversed once and traversal times will be equal to changing times. When spatio-temporal data are obsolete, both huge spatial data calculation and a great many traversal operations will impact the execution efficiency.

3. A time-based integration method

In order to refine the above shortcomings, a time-based integration method is proposed on the basis of the modeling thinking of the Event-oriented Spatio-temporal Data Model (ESTDM) advocated by Peuquet and Duan [14]. In the time-based integration method, time, which is regarded as independent time sequence rather than attribute, becomes the organizational basis for recording changes. Meanwhile, the changing process of every spatial entity will be recorded in the order of the time sequence.

3.1. Organization structure of spatio-temporal data

The integration method has established three tables including the time information table, the event information table and the cadastral object information table to record information of three basic elements (time, event and cadastral object) respectively. In addition, relationships of three elements are established by using changing relationship table, which explicitly records the changing processes of each object and implicitly records the evolving process of objects with inheritance relationship so as to bring convenience to the backtracking query of historical data.

â€¢ Time information table

In this method, time is no longer regarded as attribute, but an independent element managed by time information table. Each time is abstracted as a specific temporal object corresponding to a record in the table. Every object owns its unique identifier, which corresponds to the effective time change occurs. Thus, each time tuple can be expressed as formula (5). (5)Time(i)=f(TID,ETi)i=(1,2,â€¦,n).

In the formula (5), TID is the unique identifier of Time(i), and can be coded in accordance with some principles; ETi is the effective time the changes occur.

â€¢ Event information table

This table is used to record the changing event information, in which every tuple corresponds to an event occurring at a specifically time. Whenever a change happens, a new event record will be stored. Each event tuple can be expressed as formula (6). (6)Event(i)=f(EID,EAi,EIi)i=(1,2,â€¦,n).

In the formula (6), EID is the unique identifier of Event(i), and can be coded in accordance with some principles; EAi expresses the type of Event(i), such as segmentation, mergence and so on; EIi is the description information of Event(i).

â€¢ Cadastral object information table

Cadastral entities are also abstracted as objects, and every object owns its unique identifier whose geometric and attribute data are stored in the same record. The cadastral object information table is divided into two types in order to store current and historical data respectively. The status quo table records information of all objects at the existing state, but the objects in history table are at the non-existing but with history state. Whenever objects change, new objects will be recorded in status quo table, correspondingly, the historical objects will be recorded in history table.

It should be noted that, events are divided into two types, which are evolving event and extinction event. The unique identifier of objects will not change when evolving events happen, therefore, the objects owning the same identifier may exist in the history table. In order to distinguish the same objects but at the different historical stages, this paper makes use of the objectâ€™s identifier and the unique identifier of the event which causes the object to disappear to express the uniqueness of historical objects. Thus, each object tuple in the status quo table and history table can be respectively expressed as formula (7) and formula (8). (7)Object(i)=f(ObjID,ObjSi,ObjAi)i=(1,2,â€¦,n)(8)Object(i)=f(ObjID,EIDi,ObjSi,ObjAi)i=(1,2,â€¦,n).

In the formula (7), (8), ObjID is the unique identifier of Object(i), and remains unchanged through its life cycle; ObjSi expresses the geometric location of Object(i); ObjAi is the attribute characteristics of Object(i), and may contain multiple fields; EIDi is the identifier of the event which leads to the disappearance of Object(i).

â€¢ Changing relationship table

The spatio-temporal changing process of cadastral objects can be expressed by the relationship table. Based on the following strategies, the study utilizes static relationship table to show the dynamic spatio-temporal changing process: (a) if the extinction event occurs, such as segmentation, mergence, complex change, create the changing records of newly added cadastral object to reflect their spatio-temporal changing process and utilize event marking to link different cadastral objects with inheritance relationship; (b) if the evolving event occurs, such as boundary adjustments, attribute changes, add a row of changing information to express the changing cadastral objects. Thus, each relationship tuple is expressed as formula (9). (9)Relation(i)=f(TIDi,ObjIDi,EIDi,PreEIDi)i=(1,2,â€¦,n).

In the formula (9), TIDi is the unique identifier of the time when changes occur; ObjIDi expresses the unique identifier of the corresponding changing object; EIDi is the unique identifier of the event which leads object to change; PreEIDi is used to establish the linkage of objects with inheritance relationship.

For the changing relationship table, the following points should be noted:

(1)

ObjIDi is used to record cadastral objects at the initial moment and the unique identifier of objects which come into being through extinction events.

(2)

In every record, EIDi is not void except the following two cases: (a) records of cadastral objects at the initial moment; (b) records of cadastral objects that come into being through extinction events at the initial moment.

(3)

The first record of each object which come into being through extinction events need to record PreEIDi, which notes the identifier of event causing object to appear and establishes the relation between the target object and its parent object in favor for the following backtracking query.

(4)

Changing records of each object can be stored intently to show explicitly the evolving processes of every object ordered by TIDi and ObjIDi fields.

3.2. Method summary

Through the above analysis of the organization structure of the spatio-temporal data, the author believes that the method makes improvements on time semantics and query efficiency two aspects:

(1) Achieving the independent management of time information.

Time is stored as the independent object and every time is recorded according to the order of changes. As changes happen, time sequences are also updating. Compared with the method regarding time as the attribute, the expression of time semantics is more intuitively.

(2) Providing a solution for improving backtracking query efficiency through reducing times of backtracking query.

The evolving processes of every object is stored together, thus in the process of evolution query, traversal number can regarded as the number of objects involved in the whole process rather than change times, which reduces times of gradual backtracking to a large extent.

Traversal number can be expressed: Let E={E1,E2,E3,â€¦,En} indicate a set including n cadastral objects with inheritance relationship and C={C1,C2,C3,â€¦,Cn} indicate change times of every cadastral object, thus the ratio of traversal query numbers between the time-based method and the method regarding time as attribute can be expressed as formula (10):(10)S=nC1+C2+â‹¯+Cn.

The above formula shows when only extinction events occur to n objects, the change time of each object can be expressed as one, so the value for formula will be one, in other words, the traversal numbers of the two query methods are the same; When the evolving events occurs to n objects, the value for formula will less than one, and the more evolving events occur, the value for formula will be near zero. In the process of cadastral changes, evolving events frequently occur, which may be positive, such as the property change of cadastral objects or may be passive, for example, a segmentation event triggers boundary adjustment of adjacent cadastral objects. Therefore, for cadastral objects the cadastral objects whose evolving events often occur, the integration method can effectively reduce traversal times and provide a solution of improving backtracking query efficiency.

5. Conclusion

Combining with requirements of cadastral management, this study does research on the integration method of spatio-temporal data at the spatial database level, and proposes a time-based integration method. In this method, time, as the independent time sequence, becomes the organizational basis for recording changes and express time semantics better; furthermore, this method indicates the dynamic changing process of cadastral objects through static two dimension tables. This method can express time semantics better and reduce times of backtracking query which provides a solution for improving backtracking query efficiency.
